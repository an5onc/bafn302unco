Reflection Question 1

Which investment grew the most, and why do you think it did?

Across all three portfolios, the single best-performing position at the April 8, 2026 checkpoint was QQQ — the Invesco Nasdaq-100 ETF — which turned a $10,000 investment made on January 5, 2021 into $19,437.31, a total return of 94.37% and an annualized return (CAGR) of 13.48% over the 5.25-year holding period.

Among individual stocks, Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B) — Paige's assigned stock — was the most impressive performer in the project. It turned $10,000 into $21,089.81, more than doubling over the tracking period with a CAGR of 15.26%. That made BRK.B the single highest-ending-value position across all three portfolios.

QQQ's strength came from its heavy concentration in the largest technology and growth companies — Apple, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Alphabet, Amazon, and Meta make up most of its index. When those companies recovered sharply from their 2022 rate-hike lows and surged on artificial intelligence investment themes in 2023 and 2024, QQQ captured almost all of that upside. Unlike Tesla or EA, QQQ is not dependent on any one company's earnings or decisions; if one company in the index struggles, others absorb the weight.

Berkshire Hathaway's performance tells a different story. BRK.B has no exposure to speculative technology and pays no dividend. Instead, Berkshire compounded quietly through insurance underwriting, BNSF railroad operations, Berkshire Hathaway Energy, and a portfolio of public equities managed with exceptional discipline. The company also executed billions in share buybacks during the period, which mechanically increased per-share value. BRK.B demonstrates that high returns do not require high volatility — Berkshire's path was far steadier than any of the growth-heavy positions.

Reflection Question 2

Which investment was the most stable, and what does that tell you about risk?

The most stable investment in the project was AGG — the iShares Core U.S. Aggregate Bond ETF. AGG did not behave like the equity positions. Its price moved in a much tighter range and did not swing wildly in response to earnings reports, CEO decisions, or quarterly delivery numbers. However, AGG's stability worked against it in one important way: it was the only position in the project still below its original cost at the checkpoint, ending at $8,399.44 — a loss of $1,600.56 (−16.01%).

This result teaches a counterintuitive lesson about risk. "Stable" does not automatically mean "safe" in the sense of protecting your purchasing power. AGG's price fell primarily because of the Federal Reserve's rate-hiking cycle in 2022. Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions: when the Fed raised rates by 425 basis points in a single year, bond prices fell across the board. AGG, which holds thousands of U.S. investment-grade bonds, fell with them. The stability that made AGG less volatile than Tesla or QQQ could not protect it from systematic interest-rate risk.

Among the equity positions, SPY behaved as the next-most-stable holding. SPY tracks the S&P 500 — 500 companies across every sector — so it smooths out company-specific shocks. When Tesla fell 65% in 2022, SPY fell far less because the losses were offset by hundreds of other companies. SPY delivered an 81.77% total return (CAGR of 12.05%) with far less drama than Tesla or even QQQ.

The broader lesson: risk shows up in different forms. AGG carried interest-rate risk. Tesla carried company-specific execution risk. QQQ and VUG carried concentration risk in the growth sector. SPY carried market risk but spread it widely. A portfolio with all five captures all those risk types — but in doing so, it also captures different sources of return, which is exactly the point of diversification.

Reflection Question 3

How did AGG behave when stocks were volatile, and did it play the role bonds are supposed to play?

The traditional case for including a bond fund in a portfolio is that bonds tend to rise when stocks fall — providing a buffer during equity downturns. This is called the "negative correlation" between stocks and bonds, and it has historically made bonds a reliable stabilizing force in balanced portfolios.

During the 2022 market downturn, that relationship broke down. The S&P 500 fell approximately 18% in 2022. Under the traditional model, AGG should have risen to partially offset those losses. Instead, AGG fell sharply alongside equities — losing roughly 13% in calendar year 2022 alone. The reason was the same for both: rising interest rates. Rate increases hurt growth stocks by making future earnings worth less in today's dollars. They simultaneously hurt bond prices by making existing bonds' fixed coupons worth less than newly issued bonds at higher rates. When the Fed moved aggressively, both asset classes declined at once.

This was a historically unusual period. The stock-bond correlation that most investors learned about in textbooks flipped positive — stocks and bonds moved together downward — for the first time in a generation. AGG did not play the traditional diversifying role that bonds are supposed to play during an equity selloff in 2022.

However, AGG still had value in the project for a different reason: it made the overall portfolio less volatile on a daily and monthly basis throughout the entire holding period. Even though it ended below cost, it reduced the emotional swings that come with watching a purely equity portfolio. Holding AGG alongside four equity positions meant the worst days in the stock market caused less total portfolio damage. That trade-off — less upside in exchange for less downside volatility — is what bonds are actually providing, and AGG demonstrated it clearly.

Reflection Question 4

If this were real money, what would have surprised you most about how the portfolio performed?

The most surprising finding — for all three of us — was how much the individual stock assignment separated the three portfolios despite sharing four out of five holdings. All three portfolios were built on the same ETF foundation (SPY, QQQ, VUG, AGG), yet Paige's portfolio ended at $85,392.13 while Anson's ended at $78,310.35 — a difference of over $7,000 purely because Berkshire Hathaway outperformed Tesla by a wide margin as the fifth position. That gap represents a material real-world difference over five years, and it came from a single stock selection.

For Anson specifically, the most surprising thing would have been how Tesla — the stock that seemed most exciting at the time of purchase — ended up delivering the weakest return of the five positions in the portfolio. Tesla returned 40.08% over the holding period, which sounds strong in isolation, but it trailed SPY (81.77%), QQQ (94.37%), VUG (82.89%), and even Berkshire Hathaway (110.90%). The ETFs that seemed "boring" compared to a high-growth EV company actually outperformed it over the full period.

For Mikey, the most surprising element would have been EA's relative underperformance. EA entered the period as a major game publisher with steady earnings, and it still produced a 44.35% gain — but it also trailed the market-tracking ETFs by a wide margin. A company-specific decision — reducing its financial guidance in 2024 and facing a changing gaming landscape — contributed to EA lagging well behind what a simple index fund would have provided.

The lesson that unites all three reflections is a classic one: it is very difficult to select individual stocks that consistently beat a diversified index. Over this specific five-year period, QQQ and SPY — funds that require no individual company analysis, no CEO tracking, and no earnings-call monitoring — outperformed Tesla and EA (and nearly matched Berkshire) simply by holding hundreds of companies and letting the market compound. If this were real money, the most honest takeaway is that the ETF core of each portfolio did more of the work than the individual stock choice in two of the three cases — and only Paige's BRK.B managed to genuinely outperform the index funds she was paired with.
